Conquer AI
Pitch Script · v1
Internal · Presenters only
Series A · Introduction Call

The thirty-minute pitch, section by section.

Talk-track for Jonny, Tom, Dan and Jeremy. Not a script to memorise — key points, the lines we want to actually land, who takes the hand-off, and the questions partners will push on. Twenty-four minutes of content, six for their questions. If they interrupt early, let them — Q&A is a flex buffer and reclaims whatever we run over.

How to use Bullets are the spine. Italic lines in quotes are the phrasing we've workshopped — say it or something close to it.
Stage direction Red arrows (►) mark hand-offs, screen-share cues, and delivery notes. Follow them — the rhythm matters.
Rules of the room No slides until the DreamStack walkthrough — that is the only visual. Q&A (06) is our flex buffer: time we save earlier rolls into it; time we borrow gets reclaimed from it.
01
Welcome & introductions
LeadJonny Ward
2min
Talk track
  • Warm open, acknowledge their time. Name who's in the room and why — one sentence per person, not a CV.
  • Set the shape of the call out loud: twenty minutes of us, ten of them. Invite interruption early and often.
  • Flag that we'll share one screen once — the DreamStack walkthrough. Everything else is conversation.
  • Don't over-explain the intros. Partners have read the teaser. The goal is to get to the thesis fast.
Jonny opens. Quick round-table: Tom, Dan, Jeremy each say one line — their role + the part of the call they'll lead.
Say it like this
Thanks for making time. We've structured the next half hour so you get the full picture in twenty minutes, with ten at the end for questions. Interrupt whenever something lands — or doesn't.
I'm Jonny, CEO. With me are Tom on the tech, Dan on the numbers, and Jeremy — our chairman — who'll come in on the advisory network.
Smile. Don't rush. Let the room settle before Jonny opens the thesis and hands to Tom for the market-shift beat.
02
The thesis — why now
LeadJonny Ward & Tom Buckley
3min
Talk track
  • Name the market shift in one line: every enterprise is being asked to become AI-native, and almost none of them know how. That gap is the opportunity.
  • The infrastructure layer for this shift is being chosen now — not in three years. Whoever owns the rails for the next eighteen months defines the category.
  • Existing "AI platforms" are either model wrappers (no moat) or consulting shops (no scale). Neither is the shape of the winner.
  • The winner looks like DreamStack: a product that captures intent, turns it into a running system, and compounds with every deployment.
  • Close the section on urgency. The window is eighteen months. After that, the enterprise buyers have picked their platform and the story is distribution, not innovation.
Jonny opens with the market shift and urgency. Tom takes the second half — why existing "AI platforms" fail and the shape of the winner — so partners hear the CTO name the category, not just the CEO. Slow down. Don't hedge. If you feel yourself qualifying, stop and re-assert.
Say it like this
Every enterprise on earth has been handed a mandate to become AI-native. Almost none of them know how. That gap — between the mandate and the method — is a generational infrastructure opportunity, and the rails are getting chosen in the next eighteen months.
The market has two flavours of answer today: thin wrappers on top of someone else's model, and consulting shops dressed up as platforms. Neither scales. The winner is a product company that owns the path from intent to running system. That's what we've built.
Jonny lands the eighteen months line and pauses — then hands to Tom for the "two flavours" beat. Tom: speak as the builder, not the salesman. If a partner nods, you've sold the urgency — move on.
Why is the window eighteen months, specifically?
Enterprise procurement cycles for platform decisions are 12–18 months. The Fortune 2000 are mid-cycle now. By FY26 those choices are signed and the buying pattern flips from evaluation to expansion.
Isn't OpenAI / Anthropic / the hyperscalers going to eat this?
They own the model and the compute. They don't own the enterprise implementation layer — and they've shown no appetite to. Every platform win in the last cycle was the company sitting on top of the primitive, not the primitive itself. Snowflake on top of S3, Stripe on top of card rails. Same pattern.
03
DreamStack — the product
LeadTom Buckley
8min
Structure // three stages, two minutes each, leave 2 min to breathe and handle inline questions
  • Dream Capture. A guided session that turns an ambiguous business problem into a structured specification. Not a form, not a chatbot — a collaborative interview that produces an artefact the rest of the stack consumes.
  • Dream Studio. The canvas where that specification becomes a working system — data flows, agents, policies, integrations — assembled from reusable components, reviewable by humans at every step.
  • Dream Factory. The runtime. Deploys, monitors, versions, and rolls back. It's where the customer's AI actually lives, and where we compound — every deployment makes the next one faster.
Tom takes over from Jonny. Share screen now — DreamCatcher session open on screen one, Studio canvas on screen two, Factory dashboard on screen three. Do not read the slide; narrate the product.
Say it like this
Most "AI platforms" give you a model and a blank page. We give you three things: a conversation that produces a spec, a canvas that turns the spec into a system, and a runtime that ships it. Capture, Studio, Factory.
Here's a live one — a mid-market insurer. Thirty minutes in Capture. Four hours in Studio. Live in production the same week. Every artefact you see on screen came out of that session.
Proof points to drop
  • Week-one deployments. Current average Capture-to-Production is 6.2 days. Target for Q3 is under three.
  • Component reuse. 71% of Studio components are reused across customers. That ratio is the moat.
  • Factory telemetry. Every production system phones home — we see what works, we ship it back into the catalogue. Compounding.
If a partner leans in during the demo, stop and dig in. The demo is the pitch. Don't rush to the financials.
How is this different from [Palantir / C3 / Databricks AI]?
Palantir is a services company with a product wrapper — two-year deployments, $10m+ contracts. C3 is an application catalogue. Databricks is the data plane underneath us, not a competitor. We're the product company for the path from business problem to running system, at mid-market price points.
04
Traction & the advisory network
LeadJonny Ward & Jeremy Amias
4min
Talk track — Jonny (90s)
  • Current ARR, growth trajectory, logo count. Name the customers we can name. For the ones we can't, describe the industry + scale.
  • Win rate in qualified pipeline. Time-to-close. Land-and-expand multiples on the accounts that have been with us > 6 months.
  • Source of pipeline — this is the lead-in to Jeremy's advisor story. Where does it actually come from?
Jonny: after the pipeline-source sentence, literally turn to Jeremy. "This is where Jeremy comes in."
Talk track — Jeremy (2.5 min)
  • The advisory network: senior operators at enterprise buyers, compensated in equity, who introduce us into their own organisations and peer networks.
  • Current roster — 14 advisors, covering FS, insurance, healthcare, defence, public sector. Six are CIOs at Fortune-500s.
  • Why it compounds: each advisor sources 3–5 qualified intros per quarter; each landed logo recruits the next advisor. The network is the go-to-market.
  • Why this is the moat partners under-price: it can't be copied by a company that raised last week. It took us two years and a founding team with these relationships to build.
Jeremy: speak slowly. This section is your credibility, not a sales pitch. Partners will probe it — let them.
Say it like this
Every dollar of ARR we've booked came from someone in our advisor network making the introduction. It's not a marketing channel. It's the business model.
What happens to the advisor model at scale? Doesn't it cap out?
It scales in two dimensions. First, each advisor's network is itself recursive — they bring their successors. Second, once we're inside a category, the customer reference becomes the next advisor. We're seeing the flywheel now, not in theory.
How are advisors compensated, and is the equity dilution material?
Modest equity grants on a four-year vest, cliff at one year, tied to performance. Total advisor pool is under 3% fully-diluted. Dan can walk through the cap table on request.
05
Business model, financials & the ask
LeadDan Moon & Jonny Ward
5min
Talk track — Dan (3 min)
  • Three-tier pricing. Capture sessions sold standalone at low ACV (the land). Studio + Factory at mid-six-figures committed ARR (the expand). Enterprise platform deals at seven figures (the scale).
  • Unit economics. Gross margin 74% today, tracking to 82% at scale as Factory amortises. CAC payback 11 months. Net revenue retention 138%.
  • Path to profitability. Default-alive at the end of the A. Profitable at current burn within 18 months on current pipeline conversion.
  • Year-4 trajectory. $120m ARR base case, $200m bull case. Burn multiple < 1.0 from month 12 post-A.
Dan: tight, numeric, no hedging. Three minutes — do not drift. Partners want to see the model runs. If they ask to see the spreadsheet, say yes — do not send it live, send it after. Hand cleanly to Jonny on the Y4 line.
Talk track — Jonny (2 min)
  • Round size, valuation, structure. Direct, no dance.
  • Use of funds. GTM scale-up (50%), product (30%), team buildout (20%). Name the specific hires that unlock the next twelve months.
  • What a lead looks like. Size of cheque, board seat, expectation on timeline. Be specific so we don't waste a cycle on a non-lead.
  • Why them. One line — genuine, not flattery. Something about their portfolio, a partner's thesis, a past bet that rhymes with ours.
Jonny: look at the lead partner when you say "what a lead looks like." Make eye contact. This is the moment of the call.
Say it like this
Three tiers. Capture is how we get in — low-friction, five-figure entry. Studio is where the customer commits. Factory is where they stay. NRR at 138 today.
We're not asking you to underwrite a story where the economics show up later. The economics are here now. What the round buys is speed.
Dan closes on the Y4 line. Jonny picks up with the ask — same breath, no pause, the numbers and the ask are one story.
We're raising a $40m Series A at a $220m post. We're looking for a lead writing $15–25m and taking a board seat. We want this wrapped in six weeks so we can get back to shipping.
Use of funds: half to GTM — specifically, four enterprise AEs and two advisor-network operators. Thirty percent to product — the Factory autonomous-deployment roadmap that doubles deployment throughput. Twenty percent to the team — a VP Eng and a head of security.
138 NRR from how many cohorts?
Three full cohorts now past their 12-month mark. Sample is 40 logos. We don't have enough data to call the number stable below 100 logos — flagging that honestly.
What's the model's assumption for pricing compression from hyperscalers?
Modelled at 18% annual compression at the Capture tier, zero at the Factory tier (switching cost). Even with 30% compression across the board the model clears $80m by Y4.
What's the rest of the round look like — do you have soft commits?
Existing investors are pro-rata. We have two strategic soft commits at $5m each. $25m is uncommitted and reserved for the lead and co-lead.
06
Your questions & discussion
LeadAll three — Jonny fields
6min
How to run Q&A // this section is our flex buffer — time saved rolls in, time borrowed is reclaimed here
  • Jonny fields first. Decides who answers. Don't answer something that isn't yours — hand off. A confident hand-off is more impressive than a generalist answer.
  • Shortest honest answer wins. If you don't know, say so and say when you'll follow up. Do not speculate in a Series A meeting.
  • Don't sell in Q&A. The pitch is over. Answer, land, move to the next question.
  • Watch the clock. Q&A is floored at six minutes but flexes up if we finished early. If we're at the four-minute mark and they're still asking, that's a buying signal — do not cut them off.
If the conversation is generative, stay in it. Tom — watch the clock so Jonny doesn't have to. If the flex-buffer ledger went red, we ran over earlier; protect the last two minutes for Jonny's next-steps close.
Anticipated questions we haven't covered
What's your moat against a well-funded fast-follower?
Three layers. The advisor network (relational, non-replicable). The component catalogue and telemetry loop (compounding, data-gated). The founding team's prior relationships in regulated industries (time-gated). A competitor can copy the product in 18 months; none of the three layers in under three years.
How do you think about building vs buying distribution?
We don't buy distribution. The advisor model is our distribution. Partnerships with the big SIs are on the roadmap as delivery partners once we're at Factory scale — not as a customer-acquisition channel.
What keeps you up at night?
Two things, honestly. Hiring speed — the top of the AE funnel in this market is brutal. And Factory reliability as deployments scale into the hundreds. Both are solvable, both are in the use-of-funds, both are why we're raising.
Why haven't you raised more already?
Deliberate. We wanted to prove the model — the advisor network, the unit economics, the week-one deployments — before scaling the round. We're raising now because the market window is closing, not because we ran out of runway.
What does the team look like in 18 months?
From 42 today to 90–100. Roughly a third engineering, a third GTM, the rest spread across customer operations, security, and the advisor network team. No consulting arm — ever.

Rules of engagement
  • Time is sacred. Twenty-four minutes of material, six for them. Q&A (06) is the flex buffer — it shrinks if we run long, grows if we finish tight.
  • Hand off cleanly. Name the person, pause, let them pick up. No talking over each other.
  • Don't qualify. No "sort of," no "kind of," no "we think." We either know or we don't.
  • Silence is fine. After a big line, let it sit. Do not fill the pause.
  • Listen for the real question. Most partner questions are actually about risk. Name the risk they're pointing at.
Things we do not say
  • "We're like [X] for [Y]." Ever.
  • "It's early." It is. They know. Don't remind them.
  • "AI-powered." Everything is. Say what it actually does.
  • "Skip section 08." There is no section 08 any more. The script is 07.
  • "We're exploring…" Either we're doing it or we're not.
  • Competitor names unless they ask. Then be specific and generous.
